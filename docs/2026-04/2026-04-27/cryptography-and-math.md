Is “cryptography” a subset of “mathematics”?

## Cryptography and Math

:::note

Source: Grok

:::

**Yes**, cryptography is a subset (or more precisely, a subfield or applied branch) of mathematics.

### Why?
Cryptography fundamentally relies on mathematical structures and principles to achieve its goals: confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and non-repudiation. Core areas include:

- **Number theory**: Prime numbers, modular arithmetic, integer factorization, discrete logarithms (e.g., RSA, Diffie-Hellman, elliptic curve cryptography).
- **Algebra**: Finite fields (Galois fields), group theory, ring theory, and lattice-based problems (used in post-quantum cryptography).
- **Probability and statistics**: Randomness, entropy, statistical tests for pseudorandom generators, side-channel analysis.
- **Combinatorics and information theory**: Coding theory, complexity theory (e.g., NP-hard problems for security reductions), hash functions.
- **Geometry and algebraic geometry**: Elliptic curves, pairings (used in pairing-based cryptography).

Modern cryptography is built on **provable security**—reducing the security of a scheme to the hardness of a well-defined mathematical problem (e.g., "If you can break this cryptosystem, you can solve this hard math problem"). This makes it deeply mathematical.

### Nuances
- **Not all of cryptography is pure math**: It also draws from computer science (algorithms, complexity, implementation), engineering (hardware, side-channels, performance), and even physics (quantum cryptography, random number generation from physical sources). Practical cryptography involves software engineering, protocol design, and standards (e.g., TLS, AES).
- **Historical roots**: Early cryptography was more linguistic or mechanical (e.g., Caesar cipher, Enigma), but since the 20th century—especially with Shannon's information-theoretic foundations and public-key crypto in the 1970s—it became rigorously mathematical.

## What is arithmatic?

**Arithmetic** (note the correct spelling: **arithmetic**) is the branch of **mathematics** that deals with the basic operations on **numbers**.

### Simple Definition
Arithmetic is the study and use of:
- **Addition** (+)
- **Subtraction** (−)
- **Multiplication** (×)
- **Division** (÷)

...and how these operations work with different types of numbers (whole numbers, fractions, decimals, negative numbers, etc.).

### What It Covers
Arithmetic includes:
- Basic calculations (e.g., 7 + 5 = 12)
- Properties of numbers (commutative, associative, distributive)
- Order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS)
- Fractions, decimals, percentages
- Ratios and proportions
- Powers and roots (exponents)
- Working with integers (positive and negative numbers)
- Mental math and estimation

### How It Relates to Mathematics
Arithmetic is the **most fundamental** and **elementary** part of mathematics.  
Think of it like this:
- Arithmetic = the “tools” and “building blocks”
- The rest of mathematics (algebra, geometry, calculus, number theory, cryptography, etc.) builds on top of arithmetic.

You use arithmetic every day — when you calculate change at a store, split a bill, measure ingredients for cooking, or check your speed while driving.

### Common Confusion
- **Arithmetic** is **not** the same as **Mathematics**.  
  Mathematics is much broader (it includes algebra, geometry, trigonometry, statistics, calculus, logic, cryptography, and more).  
  Arithmetic is just the starting point.

- People often misspell it as “arithmatic” — the correct spelling is **arithmetic** (from Greek “arithmos” meaning “number”).

### Example
Arithmetic: 15 + 27 = 42, or 8 × 6 = 48  
Algebra (next level): Solve for x → 3x + 7 = 22  
Cryptography (much higher level): Uses modular arithmetic, prime factorization, etc.
