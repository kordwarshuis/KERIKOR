# Why hash public key?

You say: “But instead of publishing B, you publish only a commitment to it: H(B) = hash/digest of B’s public key material” 

Why is the hashing necessary? Would creating a new public / private pair not be enough and make the public key available? Since you can't calculate the private key from the public key. When you pre-rotate, the new public / private pair would be activated, but nobody knows, at that moment, the private key?

## Why hash public key?

:::note

Source: KERI Wizard

:::

Excellent question. You’ve identified the exact subtlety that makes KERI’s pre-rotation interesting.

You are absolutely right that:

> Knowing a public key does NOT reveal the private key.

So at first glance, it seems unnecessary to hide the future public key at all.

But the reason KERI hashes the next keys instead of publishing them directly is **not primarily about protecting the private key**.

It is about protecting the **future key state** from *premature exposure and attack targeting*.

The distinction is subtle but important.

---

## Short Answer

KERI hashes the next keys because:

1. It keeps future keys **unexposed and unlinkable** until needed.
2. It reduces attack surface against future control authority.
3. It enables stronger compartmentalization and privacy.
4. It prevents premature operational exposure of future key material.
5. It supports stronger long-term security assumptions.

The private key would still be safe if the public key were known — but the *future control structure* would become visible and targetable.

---

## What If KERI Published B Directly?

Suppose the inception event said:

```text
Current key: A
Next key: B
```

instead of:

```text
Current key: A
Next commitment: H(B)
```

At first this seems fine.

After all:

* attackers still don’t know private key `b`
* only the public key `B` is visible

So why care?

Because now the future key is already:

* publicly exposed
* permanently associated with the identifier
* available for cryptanalysis forever
* targetable before use
* reusable metadata for correlation

KERI intentionally avoids all of that.

---

## The Core Principle: Minimize Exposure of Future Authority

KERI treats *future control authority* as sensitive information.

Even public keys leak information:

* existence
* timing
* linkage
* future authority structure
* cryptographic material available for offline analysis forever

Hash commitments reduce this exposure dramatically.

---

## Why This Matters Cryptographically

### 1. Defense Against Future Cryptanalytic Advances

Suppose:

* today’s public-key crypto is secure
* but 15 years later, a new attack weakens the algorithm

If future public keys were already published years earlier:

* attackers have had years to collect and analyze them
* quantum or classical advances may eventually recover private keys

Pre-rotation minimizes this exposure window.

The next public key is only revealed *when it becomes active*.

So instead of:

```text
Exposure window = years
```

you get:

```text
Exposure window = current rotation period
```

This is a huge security improvement.

This is one reason KERI literature discusses post-quantum resilience properties. 

---

### 2. Compartmentalization of Key State

Publishing future keys reveals future authority structure.

Example:

```text
A -> B -> C -> D
```

If all future public keys were known ahead of time:

* an attacker learns the entire planned succession chain
* organizational control patterns become visible
* future rotations become predictable

KERI instead reveals only:

```text
H(B)
```

which says:

> “Some valid future key exists.”

without revealing what it is.

---

### 3. Reduced Metadata Correlation

Public keys are identifiers.

Publishing them early creates correlatable metadata.

Hash commitments conceal future identifiers until activation.

This aligns with KERI’s broader philosophy around minimizing exploitably correlatable metadata. 

---

### 4. Operational Security (OpSec)

If future public keys are visible:

* attackers know exactly which future keys matter
* they can target devices, HSMs, backups, personnel, or storage associated with them

Even if the crypto is mathematically secure, operational compromise becomes easier when targets are known.

Hash commitments obscure future targets.

---

### 5. Commitment Without Disclosure

This is the elegant cryptographic property KERI exploits.

A hash commitment gives two guarantees simultaneously:

#### Binding

You cannot later change the future key.

Because:

```text
hash(B) != hash(fake_B)
```

So the commitment locks you into the future key state.

#### Hiding

Nobody learns the future key itself until you reveal it.

This is classic commitment-scheme behavior.

KERI uses a lightweight cryptographic commitment model based on hashes.

---

## Another Important Point

You said:

> “When you pre-rotate, the new public/private pair would be activated, but nobody knows the private key.”

Correct.

But KERI’s goal is stronger than merely protecting the private key.

It also wants to protect:

* future authority structure
* future operational state
* future cryptographic exposure
* future metadata visibility

So KERI minimizes publication of future information wherever possible.

---

## Intuition

Think of it like this:

### Publishing the future public key

is like saying:

> “Next year I’ll move to this exact address.”

### Publishing only the hash commitment

is like saying:

> “I already know my next address and can prove later that I committed to it today, but I won’t reveal it yet.”

Both preserve control.

But the second leaks much less information.

---

## In Practice

This is why KERI stores:

```text
nxt = digest(next keys)
```

instead of:

```text
next_public_keys
```

inside events.

The next keys stay dark until activation.

That “dark future authority” model is one of KERI’s elegant security properties.
